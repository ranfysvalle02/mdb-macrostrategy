Methodology & Caveats (read me)
  • What this measures: high-frequency partial updates (increment a counter, set a timestamp) to two fields buried inside a ~32 KB document. Throughput, latency percentiles, and on-disk growth are recorded from real runs.
  • Why Postgres pays here: a ~32 KB jsonb value lives out-of-line in TOAST. MVCC means an update writes a whole new row version and rewrites the TOAST chunks, leaving dead tuples for autovacuum. MongoDB applies field-level operators ($inc/$set); WiredTiger is also an MVCC / copy-on-write engine (it does not edit bytes in place), but it avoids the full-row-rewrite-plus-vacuum cycle.
  • The fairness control: switch the Postgres strategy to normalized — promote the hot counters to real columns — and the penalty largely disappears. This is the idiomatic Postgres fix; the demo lets you prove it.
  • Scope: a single workload on a single machine via Docker. It is not a claim about analytics, joins, reads, or general superiority. Both engines compress on disk (PG TOAST / WiredTiger snappy). Your numbers will differ; that is the point — run it yourself.
All figures are measured locally — nothing here is hand-written.
